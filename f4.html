🎯 IM Consultant Skills You'll Practice

Discovery: Ask the right questions to understand the real problem, not the symptom.

Design Thinking: Iterate on solutions, always keeping the user in mind.

Communication: Explain technical IM concepts to non-technical stakeholders.

Pragmatism: Propose realistic solutions within budget, timeline, and technical constraints.

Documentation: Create clear requirements, diagrams, and specifications.

💡 Scoping Tips for Your Project

  • ✓ Start small: A tight scope = better execution. Don't try to solve everything.
  • ✓ Define success metrics: How will you know if your solution worked?
  • ✓ Know your constraints: Budget, tech, time, organizational culture all matter.
  • ✓ Involve stakeholders early: The best design is one that solves their actual problem.

Project Deliverable Framework

Your final project will include these components. This tab explains what each one is and why it matters.

📋

1. Problem Statement & Business Context

What goes here:

  • What organization or scenario are you designing for?
  • What IM problem do they face? (1-2 paragraphs)
  • Why does it matter? (Impact on operations, revenue, customer satisfaction, safety?)
  • What constraints exist? (Budget, timeline, existing tech, skills)

Example: "MediCare Clinic has 3 separate patient systems. Nurses waste 1 hour per day on manual data entry. This causes 15% of prescriptions to have errors, costing the clinic $50K/year in liability."

👥

2. Stakeholder Analysis

What goes here:

  • Who are the key stakeholders? (Users, managers, IT team, executives, customers)
  • What does each group need from the system?
  • What are their pain points?
  • How will each group be affected by your solution?

Format Suggestion: Create a simple table with columns: Stakeholder Group | Needs | Pain Points | Benefits from Solution

⚙️

3. Current State Analysis (As-Is)

What goes here:

  • What systems or processes exist today?
  • Draw a data flow diagram showing how information currently moves
  • Identify bottlenecks, redundancies, security gaps, or manual workarounds
  • What works well? What doesn't?

Visual Tip: Use simple boxes and arrows to show data flow. Label each system/process. Include decision points where data branches.

🎯

4. Functional Requirements

What goes here:

A list of what the new system MUST do. Write each as a user story or requirement:

Example Format:

• "The system SHALL display patient medical history from all departments in one unified view"

• "Users SHALL be able to search for records by patient ID, name, or insurance number"

• "The system SHALL auto-sync inventory updates every 15 minutes"

Aim for 8-12 clear requirements. Avoid vague language like "should be fast" or "user-friendly" — be specific!

🏗️

5. Proposed System Design (To-Be)

What goes here:

  • Draw a data flow diagram of your proposed solution
  • Show all systems, databases, and integrations
  • Identify what's new, what's reused, what's retired
  • Describe the technology stack (database, tools, platforms)
  • Explain why this design solves the original problem

Key Diagrams: Data Flow Diagram (DFD) or System Context Diagram. Keep it simple — boxes for systems, labeled arrows for data movement.

📅

6. Implementation Plan & Success Metrics

What goes here:

  • Timeline: Break the project into 3-4 phases with milestones
  • Resources Needed: Budget estimate, team size, expertise required
  • Risks & Mitigation: What could go wrong? How will you prevent it?
  • Success Metrics: How will you measure if the project worked?

Success Metrics Example: Reduce data entry time by 50% | Decrease prescription errors to 0% | Achieve 99.5% system uptime

It's about taking IM theories and seeing how they play out in real organizations — the wins, the failures, and the lessons.

1

Data → Information → Knowledge

Raw data becomes useful information when organized. Information becomes knowledge when people apply it to decisions.

Example: Sales numbers → Monthly trend report → Decision to restock

2

Information Systems (IS)

The combination of people, processes, and technology used to collect, store, process, and distribute information.

Example: A university's enrollment system

3

Why Case Studies?

Textbook theory is not enough. Real cases show how companies succeed or fail based on how they manage information.

You learn to think like a consultant, not just a student.

Case Study Analysis Framework

Use this 5-step process for any IM case you encounter:

1

Identify the Problem

What information management challenge does the organization face? Is it about data quality, security, access, integration, or governance?

2

Analyze Stakeholders

Who is affected? Employees, customers, management, IT teams? What are their needs and how do they interact with the information system?

3

Evaluate the System

What technology and processes are in place? What's working? What's broken? Map the information flow.

4

Propose Solutions

Recommend specific, actionable changes — new tools, better policies, restructured workflows, training programs.

5

Predict Outcomes

What happens if your solution is implemented? What are the risks? How would you measure success?

💡 Pro Tip

Always connect your analysis back to IM concepts: data governance, information lifecycle, system integration, security, and decision support. Your instructor wants to see you apply the theory.

Real-World Case Studies

Click each case to expand. These are simplified versions of real scenarios — perfect for group analysis.

Background: Metro City Hospital serves 200,000+ patients yearly. They have 3 separate information systems: one for Emergency (installed 2010), one for Outpatient (installed 2015), and one for Lab Results (installed 2018). These systems don't communicate with each other.

The Problem: A patient, Maria Santos, visited the ER with chest pain. The ER doctor prescribed a blood thinner. Two days later, she visited the outpatient clinic for a follow-up. The outpatient doctor — who couldn't see the ER records — prescribed another blood thinner, causing a dangerous overdose. Maria was rushed back to the ER.

Additional Facts:

  • Nurses manually re-enter patient data across systems (takes ~15 min per patient)
  • 23% of lab results are delayed because doctors can't access them from the outpatient system
  • The hospital's IT budget was cut by 30% this year
  • Staff have complained about "system fatigue" — too many logins and interfaces

🔍 Analysis Prompts:

  1. What type of IM problem is this? (Hint: system integration, data silos)
  2. Who are the stakeholders and what does each group need?
  3. What are 2-3 realistic solutions given the budget constraint?
  4. How would you measure if your solution worked?

Background: FreshMart is a mid-size grocery chain that launched online ordering during the pandemic. Orders grew from 50/day to 2,000/day in 6 months. Their system was originally designed for in-store inventory only.

The Problem: Customers started receiving wrong items, expired products, or missing orders. Online reviews dropped from 4.5 to 2.1 stars. Investigation revealed: the inventory system updates only once every 24 hours, so the website shows items as "in stock" that were sold out hours ago. There's no system linking online orders to warehouse picking.

Additional Facts:

  • 35% of online orders have at least one substitution or missing item
  • Warehouse staff use printed paper lists to pick orders
  • Customer service receives 300+ complaint calls daily
  • Competitor "GreenBasket" has real-time inventory and same-day delivery
  • FreshMart's CEO wants to fix this in 90 days

🔍 Analysis Prompts:

  1. Identify the root cause vs. symptoms of this problem
  2. Map the information flow from customer order → delivery. Where does it break?
  3. Propose a phased 90-day plan (what to fix first, second, third)
  4. How does poor information management affect competitive advantage?

Background: State University (25,000 students) decided to replace their 15-year-old enrollment system with a modern cloud-based platform. The migration was scheduled over summer break — 8 weeks to move everything.

The Problem: When classes started, the new system crashed on Day 1. Students couldn't enroll, see their schedules, or check grades from previous semesters. It turned out that 40% of student records had data errors after migration — wrong course codes, missing prerequisites, duplicate accounts.

Additional Facts:

  • Faculty were given only a 2-hour training session on the new system
  • The old system stored data in a format incompatible with the new one
  • No parallel testing was done (old and new systems running simultaneously)
  • Students flooded social media with complaints — local news picked up the story
  • The registrar's office processed everything manually for 3 weeks

🔍 Analysis Prompts:

  1. What went wrong in the planning and execution of this migration?
  2. Identify at least 3 IM principles that were violated
  3. If you were the CIO, what would you have done differently?
  4. How does this case illustrate the importance of change management in IM?
